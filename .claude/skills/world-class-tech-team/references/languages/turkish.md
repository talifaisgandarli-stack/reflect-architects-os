# Turkish Language Reference

Native-level Turkish across all registers. Turkish is the official language of Turkey and Northern Cyprus, and is closely related to Azerbaijani (both are Oghuz Turkic languages). A native Azerbaijani speaker can understand much Turkish but should not assume identical usage — register, vocabulary, and cultural norms differ meaningfully.

---

## 1. Register spectrum

### Resmi (Formal/Official)
Used in: state documents, official correspondence, legal contracts, formal reports
Characteristics: full grammatical forms, passive constructions, specific epistolary formulas, no colloquialisms
Example opener: "Sayın [Unvan] [Soyadı],"
Sign-off: "Saygılarımla," / "Derin saygılarımla,"

### İş / Profesyonel (Business/Professional)
Used in: business emails, proposals, client communication, meetings
Characteristics: warm, structured, "Siz" form always, titles on first contact
Example opener: "Merhaba [Ad]," or "İyi günler [Unvan] [Soyadı],"
Sign-off: "Saygılarımla," / "İyi çalışmalar,"

### Yarı-Resmi (Semi-formal)
Used in: colleague emails, professional WhatsApp, internal communication
Characteristics: friendly but professional, "Siz" or "sen" depending on relationship

### Günlük / Samimi (Conversational/Informal)
Used in: friends, close colleagues, WhatsApp, social media
Characteristics: "sen" form, contractions, slang, emoji
Example: "Naber? Dosyayı atabilir misin?"

### Argo / Gençlik Dili (Slang/Youth)
Current Turkish slang (2024-2025):
- "Ya" — universal filler/emphasis (Ya be! / Ya tamam)
- "Canım" — my dear/darling (very common, warm)
- "Moruk" — old man / dude (informal, used between friends)
- "Aga/Abi" — brother/bro (respect+casual, male)
- "Abla" — sister (respect+casual, female)
- "Efsane" — legendary/amazing
- "Süper" — super/great (direct borrowing from French/English)
- "Kafayı yemek" — to go crazy (idiom, lit. "to eat the head")
- "Çok iyi" — very good (also used as slang approval)
- "Yok artık" — no way / come on (disbelief)
- "Nasılsın be" — how are you man (friendly)
- "Kanka" — best friend/buddy (very casual)
- "Kral/Kraliçe" — king/queen (praise)
- "Harika" — wonderful/fantastic
- "Hayırlısı" — may it be for the best (common expression of acceptance)
- "Olur" — okay/sure/agreed (extremely versatile)
- Dated: "Süper aslan" (old praise phrase)

## 2. The "Siz" vs "sen" distinction

**"Siz" (formal/respectful "you"):**
- Always use with: strangers, clients, senior colleagues, elders, officials
- Always use in: formal documents, professional emails, first contact
- Turkish business culture maintains "Siz" more consistently than some other cultures

**"sen" (informal "you"):**
- Use with: close friends, family, children, age peers who initiate informality
- Switching to "sen": wait for the other party to suggest it ("Seninle sen diye konuşabilir miyiz?" — Can we use sen with each other?)

## 3. Titles and address forms

Turkish has a rich title system — using them correctly signals respect.

| Title | Usage |
|---|---|
| Sayın | Dear (formal letters, most respectful) |
| Bay | Mr. (formal) |
| Bayan | Ms./Mrs. (formal) |
| Hanım | Ms./Mrs. (common, warmer than Bayan) — "Ayşe Hanım" |
| Bey | Mr. (common, warmer than Bay) — "Ahmet Bey" |
| Hocam | My teacher/professor (also used as general respect: "Evet hocam") |
| Beyefendi | Gentleman (very formal/respectful) |
| Hanımefendi | Lady (very formal/respectful) |

**Common address patterns:**
- First name + Bey/Hanım: "Ahmet Bey", "Fatma Hanım" — standard professional
- Title + Surname: "Dr. Yılmaz", "Müdür Kaya" — more formal
- Just first name: only with "sen" relationship

## 4. Industry registers

### Hukuki (Legal)
Key terms: "sözleşme" (contract), "yükümlülük" (obligation), "hak" (right), "dava" (lawsuit/case), "mahkeme" (court), "icra" (execution/enforcement), "rehin/ipotek" (collateral/mortgage), "garanti/teminat" (guarantee), "tazminat" (compensation), "fesih" (termination), "taraflar" (the parties)
Register: highly formal, impersonal constructions, specific legal formulas

### İnşaat / Mimarlık (Construction/Architecture)
Key terms: "proje" (project), "çizim/plan" (drawing/plan), "keşif/metraj" (bill of quantities/estimate), "müteahhit" (contractor), "alt müteahhit" (subcontractor), "işveren/mal sahibi" (client/owner), "teknik denetim" (technical supervision), "hakediş" (progress payment), "geçici kabul" (provisional acceptance), "kesin kabul" (final acceptance), "imalat" (work/construction)
Register: technical; many French-origin terms in architecture (plan, facade, cephe)

### Finans (Finance)
Key terms: "gelir/hasılat" (income/revenue), "gider/harcama" (expense), "kâr" (profit), "zarar" (loss), "vergi" (tax), "KDV" (VAT — Katma Değer Vergisi), "bilanço" (balance sheet), "bütçe" (budget), "ödeme" (payment), "fatura" (invoice), "alacak" (receivable), "borç" (debt/loan)

### Teknoloji / Startup
Key terms: "yazılım" (software), "uygulama" (application), "kullanıcı" (user), "veri tabanı" (database), "arayüz" (interface), "güncelleme" (update), "geliştirici" (developer), "ürün" (product), "girişim" (startup/venture)
Register: Turkish tech community mixes Turkish and English freely; English terms often used as-is

### İnsan Kaynakları / HR
Key terms: "çalışan" (employee), "maaş" (salary), "izin" (leave/vacation), "performans değerlendirmesi" (performance review), "kariyer" (career), "pozisyon/görev" (position), "işe alım" (recruitment), "iş sözleşmesi" (employment contract), "istifa" (resignation), "işten çıkarma" (dismissal)

### Gayrimenkul (Real Estate)
Key terms: "mülk" (property), "tapu" (title deed — critical Turkish concept), "kira" (rent), "satış" (sale), "konut" (residential), "ticari" (commercial), "müteahhit" (developer/contractor), "kat mülkiyeti" (apartment ownership), "devir" (transfer)

## 5. Email conventions

### Formal letter structure
1. "Sayın [Unvan] [Soyadı]," (Dear [Title] [Surname],)
2. Body: formal constructions, purpose stated clearly
3. "Saygılarımla," / "Derin saygılarımla," + Full name + Title + Organization

### Business email structure
1. "İyi günler [Ad] [Bey/Hanım]," or "Merhaba [Ad] [Bey/Hanım],"
2. Body: clear, warm, structured
3. "Saygılarımla," / "İyi çalışmalar," + Name

### WhatsApp/informal
1. "Merhaba!" / "Selam!" / "Naber?"
2. Direct and brief
3. "Teşekkürler" / "Sağ ol" / "Görüşürüz"

## 6. Turkish-Azerbaijani differences — important for Azerbaijani speakers

These languages are close but not identical. Key differences:

| Concept | Azerbaijani | Turkish | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hello | Salam | Merhaba | |
| Thank you | Təşəkkür edirəm / Sağ ol | Teşekkür ederim / Sağ ol | Similar |
| Good | Yaxşı | İyi | Different roots |
| I want | İstəyirəm | İstiyorum | Similar |
| To do | Etmək | Etmek | Same |
| Water | Su | Su | Same |
| Yes | Hə / Bəli | Evet | Different |
| No | Yox / Xeyr | Hayır / Yok | Partly similar |

**False friends:** Words that look similar but mean different things:
- "Çox" (Azerbaijani: very/many) vs "Çok" (Turkish: very/many) — same meaning
- "Var" (Azerbaijani: exists/there is) = "Var" (Turkish: same) — same
- "Bilmək" (Azerbaijani: to know) = "Bilmek" (Turkish: same) — same

## 7. Cultural communication norms

**Hospitality is sacred.** Offering tea/coffee is mandatory. Refusing hospitality is a mild insult. "Çay içer misiniz?" (Would you like tea?) must almost always be accepted.

**Hierarchy matters.** Turkey is more hierarchical than Western Europe. Address superiors with titles. Wait to be invited to use first names.

**Relationship before business.** Not as extreme as Gulf Arabic, but Turkish business culture expects some relationship building before getting to the deal. Small talk is functional, not decorative.

**"Tamam" is the most versatile word.** Okay / understood / agreed / I hear you. Learn to hear its different meanings.

**"İnşallah" in Turkish.** Used similarly to Arabic but somewhat less loaded with religious weight in secular Turkish contexts. Can mean everything from genuine intention to polite deflection.

**Directness varies by generation and region.** Istanbul business culture is direct and modern. Anatolian business culture is warmer and more relationship-based.

## 8. Common errors non-natives make in Turkish

- Wrong vowel harmony (Turkish vowel harmony is strict — suffixes must harmonize with root vowels)
- Wrong consonant mutation (p→b, ç→c, t→d at certain morphological boundaries)
- Missing or wrong case suffixes (Turkish has 6 cases)
- Using "sen" too early with strangers or seniors
- Word order: Turkish is SOV (Subject-Object-Verb) — the verb almost always comes last
- Forgetting buffer vowels in compound suffixes
- Confusing "de/da" (and) with "de/da" (also/too) with "de/da" locative case
- Overusing the word "güzel" (beautiful) — native speakers vary their adjectives much more
