# English Language Reference

Native-level English across all registers, industries, and dialects. English is the lingua franca of global business — but "business English" is not one thing. Knowing which flavour, which register, and which industry conventions to apply is what separates native from near-native.

---

## 1. Register spectrum

### Formal
Used in: legal documents, contracts, official correspondence, academic writing, formal reports
Characteristics: full sentences, no contractions, passive voice acceptable, Latinate vocabulary preferred
Example: "We write to inform you that the aforementioned agreement has been executed."

### Professional / business
Used in: emails, proposals, presentations, client communication
Characteristics: clear, warm, direct, contractions acceptable, active voice preferred
Example: "I wanted to follow up on our conversation last week and share a few thoughts."

### Neutral / editorial
Used in: articles, blog posts, documentation, general writing
Characteristics: clear, readable, no jargon unless audience is technical
Example: "The platform handles payroll, project tracking, and client management in one place."

### Casual / conversational
Used in: Slack, WhatsApp, internal chat, casual emails between colleagues
Characteristics: fragments acceptable, contractions common, filler words fine
Example: "Hey — any updates on the brief? Need it by EOD if possible."

### Slang / informal
Used in: social media, youth communication, very informal settings
Current slang (2024-2025): "lowkey", "no cap", "slay", "bussin", "it's giving", "understood the assignment", "main character energy", "rent free" (living rent free in my head)
Dated slang to avoid: "on fleek", "YOLO" in serious contexts, "bae" in professional settings

## 2. British vs American vs Australian differences

### Spelling
| American | British | Australian |
|---|---|---|
| organize | organise | organise |
| color | colour | colour |
| center | centre | centre |
| analyze | analyse | analyse |
| license (n+v) | licence (n) / license (v) | licence (n) / license (v) |

### Vocabulary
| American | British | Australian |
|---|---|---|
| apartment | flat | flat/apartment |
| elevator | lift | lift |
| vacation | holiday | holiday |
| resume | CV | CV/resume |
| attorney | solicitor/barrister | solicitor/barrister |
| cell phone | mobile | mobile |
| gotten | got | got |

### Tone differences
- **American business English:** warmer, more enthusiastic ("Absolutely!", "Great question!", "I'd love to...")
- **British business English:** more understated, indirect refusals ("I wonder if we might consider...", "That's quite interesting")
- **Australian business English:** informal even in professional settings, egalitarian ("No worries", "Cheers", first names immediately)

## 3. Industry registers

### Legal
Key phrases: "hereinafter referred to as", "notwithstanding", "pursuant to", "indemnify and hold harmless", "in witness whereof", "force majeure", "time is of the essence"
Avoid: ambiguity, contractions, passive voice that obscures responsibility

### Architecture / construction
Key terms: "scope of work", "bill of quantities", "practical completion", "defects liability period", "contractor/subcontractor/client", "design intent", "as-built drawings", "shop drawings", "RFI (Request for Information)", "snagging"
Register: technical but clear; drawings speak louder than words

### Finance / accounting
Key terms: "P&L (profit and loss)", "EBITDA", "accounts receivable/payable", "amortization", "depreciation", "working capital", "cash flow", "VAT/GST", "accruals basis"
Register: precise, no ambiguity, numbers always clear

### Technology / startups
Key terms: "MVP", "iterate", "pivot", "runway", "burn rate", "ARR/MRR", "churn", "DAU/MAU", "product-market fit", "tech stack", "deploy", "ship", "PR (pull request)"
Register: casual even in writing; speed and clarity valued over formality

### Real estate
Key terms: "due diligence", "title deed", "conveyancing", "completion date", "exchange of contracts", "freehold/leasehold", "LTV (loan-to-value)", "yield", "capital appreciation"

### HR / recruitment
Key terms: "onboarding", "KPIs", "OKRs", "performance review", "redundancy/layoff", "notice period", "garden leave", "NDA", "non-compete"

## 4. Email conventions

### Structure (professional)
1. Subject line: specific, actionable (not "Hello" or "Following up")
2. Opener: reference context ("Following our call yesterday..." / "I hope this finds you well" — use sparingly)
3. Body: one idea per paragraph, short paragraphs
4. CTA: clear single ask ("Could you confirm by Friday?")
5. Sign-off: "Best regards" (formal), "Best" (professional), "Thanks" (casual), "Cheers" (British/Australian casual)

### Subject line principles
- Bad: "Question", "Following up", "Hello"
- Good: "Q3 invoice — payment due 15 Nov", "Design review feedback — action needed", "Proposal v2 attached"

## 5. Common errors that reveal non-native speakers

- "Please do the needful" — Indian English, sounds odd to British/American ears
- "Kindly revert" (meaning "reply") — Indian English; use "please respond"
- "I am writing to you regarding..." — overly formal opener; use "I wanted to reach out about..."
- Double subjects: "The project it is delayed" — omit "it"
- "We are in receipt of your letter" — dated; use "We received your letter"
- "As per" — technically correct but sounds bureaucratic; use "As" or "According to"
- Overusing "utilize" when "use" works
- "Please find attached herewith" — "Please find attached" is enough

---

## 6. Culturally sensitive expressions

- Avoid: "As you know..." (implies the reader is ignorant if they don't)
- Avoid: "Obviously..." (condescending)
- Avoid: "To be honest..." (implies you weren't honest before)
- Use: "I'd like to share..." instead of "I want to tell you..."
- In British English: never open with "Hey" to a senior person
- In American English: "I look forward to connecting" is standard; in British English it can sound hollow
