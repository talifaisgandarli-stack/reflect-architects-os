# Arabic Language Reference

Native-level Arabic across Modern Standard Arabic (MSA/Fusha) and all major Middle Eastern dialects. Arabic is the highest-context language of the five — relationship markers, politeness formulas, and religious expressions are load-bearing, not decorative. Getting the wrong dialect or wrong register for the wrong audience is a significant error.

---

## 1. The MSA vs Dialect divide — critical understanding

**Modern Standard Arabic (MSA / الفصحى / Fusha):**
- Used in: formal writing, news media, official documents, contracts, academic texts, religious texts
- Understood by all educated Arabic speakers across the region
- Nobody speaks MSA natively in conversation — it's a written/formal register
- Using MSA in casual conversation sounds strange and overly formal

**Spoken dialects (العامية / Ammiya):**
- Used in: all spoken conversation, WhatsApp, social media (increasingly), informal writing
- NOT mutually intelligible across all regions — an Egyptian may not fully understand Moroccan dialect
- Choose dialect based on the audience's nationality/region

**Rule:** Formal documents and professional written communication → MSA. Authentic personal communication, WhatsApp, building real relationships → correct regional dialect.

## 2. Dialect map — Gulf, Levantine, Egyptian, Iraqi, Maghrebi

### Gulf Arabic (الخليجي) — UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, Oman
**Key features:**
- "ch" sound where MSA has "k" in some dialects: "chayf" (كيف — how are you)
- "ش" (sh) used for negation: "ما شفته" (I didn't see him)
- "وين" (wayn) — where (vs MSA "أين")
- "شلونك" (shloonak/m) / "شلونج" (shloonych/f) — how are you (Gulf)
- "زين" (zayn) — good/fine (Gulf)
- "يبه" (yuba) — dear/man (endearment, Gulf)
- "والله" (wallah) — by God / I swear / really (universal but heavy in Gulf)
- Business: formal and relationship-first; titles extremely important (Sheikh, Dr., Eng.)

**Saudi sub-dialect note:** Najdi (Riyadh) vs Hijazi (Jeddah/Mecca) differ — Hijazi is softer and more cosmopolitan.

**Emirati note:** UAE Arabic is influenced by many nationalities; Emiratis appreciate genuine effort with dialect even from non-Arabs.

### Levantine Arabic (الشامي) — Syria, Lebanon, Jordan, Palestine
**Key features:**
- "ق" (qaf) often pronounced as glottal stop or "ʔ": "ʔalb" (heart)
- "ش" prefix for negation or question: "شو بدك؟" (shu baddak — what do you want?)
- "شو" (shu) — what (vs MSA "ماذا")
- "هيك" (hayk) — like this
- "كتير" (kteer) — a lot (vs MSA "كثير")
- "يلا" (yalla) — let's go / come on (widely used across all dialects now)
- Lebanese Arabic is the most internationally recognizable (media, diaspora); sophisticated urban register
- Syrian Arabic considered classical among dialects

### Egyptian Arabic (المصري)
**Key features:**
- Most widely understood dialect due to Egyptian cinema and media
- "ق" (qaf) → "ʔ" (glottal stop): "ʔalb" (heart)
- "ج" → "g": "gamal" (جمال — Gamal)
- "إيه" (eyh) — what
- "فين" (fayn) — where
- "إزيك" (izzayak/m) / "إزيك" (izzayik/f) — how are you
- "تمام" (tamam) — good/okay (Egyptian but now used everywhere)
- "يعني" (ya'ni) — I mean / sort of (universal filler)
- "خلاص" (khalas) — finished / that's it / enough (universal)
- Egyptian is safest dialect for broad Middle East communication

### Iraqi Arabic (العراقي)
**Key features:**
- "ش" for negation similar to Gulf
- "گ" (g) where others use "ق" (q): "gaal" (he said)
- "بيّه" (biyya) — with me
- "شكو ماكو" (shku maku) — what's up / what's there (classic Iraqi)
- "زين" (zayn) — good (shared with Gulf)
- Distinct rhythmic patterns; considered musicaly by Arabic speakers

### Maghrebi Arabic — Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia (NOT Middle East — include for completeness)
**Note:** Maghrebi dialects are significantly different and NOT mutually intelligible with Middle Eastern Arabic. Heavy Berber and French influence. Do not use Maghrebi when targeting Middle Eastern audiences.

## 3. Register spectrum

### رسمي (Formal MSA)
Used in: contracts, official correspondence, formal reports, journalism
Opener: "حضرة السيد/السيدة المحترم/ة [الاسم]،"
Sign-off: "مع فائق الاحترام والتقدير،" / "تفضلوا بقبول فائق الاحترام،"

### مهني/تجاري (Business/Professional)
Used in: business emails, proposals, client communication
Opener: "السلام عليكم ورحمة الله وبركاته، الأخ/الأخت الكريم/ة [الاسم]،"
Sign-off: "مع خالص التحية والتقدير،"

### اجتماعي (Social/Semi-formal)
Used in: warm professional WhatsApp, relationship building
Mix of MSA formulas with dialect warmth

### عامي (Colloquial/Casual)
Used in: WhatsApp, friends, social media, real conversation

## 4. Islamic expressions — when and how to use

These are cultural expressions used by Muslims regardless of personal religiosity. Using them appropriately shows cultural competence. Forcing them inappropriately reads as performative.

| Expression | Transliteration | Meaning | When to use |
|---|---|---|---|
| السلام عليكم | As-salamu alaykum | Peace be upon you | Greeting (always appropriate with Arabs) |
| وعليكم السلام | Wa alaykum as-salam | And peace be upon you | Response to above |
| بسم الله | Bismillah | In the name of God | Starting something |
| إن شاء الله | Inshallah | If God wills | Future plans (also used as polite deflection) |
| الحمد لله | Alhamdulillah | Praise be to God | Expressing gratitude/relief |
| ماشاء الله | Mashallah | What God has willed | Admiring something (to ward off evil eye) |
| بارك الله فيك | Barakallahu feek | May God bless you | Thank you / appreciation |
| يعطيك العافية | Ya'tik al-'afiya | May God give you health | Thank you for effort/work done |

**Critical note on "Inshallah":**
To Western ears this sounds like "maybe/probably not." In Arab culture it's a genuine acknowledgment that the future is uncertain. In business context, "inshallah we'll deliver by Friday" can mean anything from "definitely" to "we'll try" depending on tone and context. When you need certainty, follow up with specific confirmation.

## 5. Industry registers

### قانوني (Legal)
Key terms: "عقد" (contract), "التزام" (obligation), "حق" (right), "دعوى" (lawsuit/claim), "محكمة" (court), "تنفيذ" (execution), "رهن" (mortgage/collateral), "ضمان" (guarantee), "تعويض" (compensation), "فسخ العقد" (contract termination)

### هندسة / معمارية (Engineering/Architecture)
Key terms: "مشروع" (project), "مخطط" (plan/drawing), "ميزانية" (budget), "مقاول" (contractor), "مقاول من الباطن" (subcontractor), "صاحب العمل/العميل" (client), "إشراف هندسي" (engineering supervision), "مرحلة" (phase), "تسليم" (handover)

### مالية (Finance)
Key terms: "دخل/إيرادات" (income/revenue), "مصروف/نفقات" (expense), "ربح" (profit), "خسارة" (loss), "ضريبة" (tax), "ضريبة القيمة المضافة" (VAT), "ميزانية" (budget/balance sheet), "فاتورة" (invoice), "مستحقات" (receivables/dues)

### عقارات (Real Estate — important in Gulf)
Key terms: "عقار" (property), "ملكية" (ownership), "إيجار" (rent), "بيع" (sale), "صك" (title deed — Saudi), "تسجيل" (registration), "مطور عقاري" (real estate developer), "وحدة" (unit)

## 6. Business culture across regions

### Gulf (UAE/Saudi)
- Relationships must be built before business is done — multiple meetings before closing
- Hierarchy is strict — address the most senior person first, always
- Business cards: accept with two hands, examine carefully before putting away
- Patience with timeline — "inshallah" is real; follow up without pressure
- Hospitality: accepting coffee/tea is important; refusing is mildly rude
- Ramadan: business pace slows; meetings often in evenings; be sensitive

### Levant (Lebanon/Jordan/Syria)
- More direct than Gulf in business style
- Lebanese business culture has strong Western influence (French)
- Relationship still matters but deals can move faster than Gulf

### Egypt
- Warmth and humor are part of business culture
- Bureaucracy is real — patience required for government dealings
- Cairo business culture is fast-paced compared to Gulf

- "وناسة" (wanasa) — fun/vibe

- "موب" (mob) — not (Gulf negation, esp. Saudi/Bahrain)
- "يبه" (yuba) — man/dude (endearment)
- "هيا" (hayya) — let's go / come on

### Levantine
- "هلق" (halla') — now (Lebanese/Syrian)
- "مناح" (mnih) — good (Syrian/Lebanese)
- "شو صاير" (shu sayyir) — what's happening
- "عبالي" ('abali) — I think/I feel like
- "موت" (mawt) — dying (of laughter, etc.) — very intense approval

### Egyptian
- "عامل إيه" ('amel eh) — how are you doing
- "ياسطا" (ya osta) — man/dude (respect term for skilled person)
- "ده كلام" (da kalam) — that's real talk / seriously
- "أيوه" (aywa) — yes (Egyptian yes, widely understood)
- "عايز/عايزة" ('ayiz/'ayza) — want (m/f)

## 8. Common errors non-natives make in Arabic

- Using MSA in casual WhatsApp — sounds like a news anchor, not a person
- Using the wrong dialect for the wrong audience
- Missing dual forms (Arabic has singular/dual/plural)
- Wrong gender agreement (Arabic has grammatical gender for all nouns)
- Missing "sun letters" and "moon letters" in al- (ال) prefix assimilation
- Overusing inshallah or religious expressions performatively
- Not using appropriate greeting formulas — jumping to business without proper greeting reads as rude
- Using Lebanese dialect with Gulf audience or vice versa without noting it
