## 1. Architectural practice

### Project phases (standard)
```
Pre-design      → Site analysis, brief, feasibility
Schematic       → Concept, massing, spatial organization
Design Dev      → Refinement, systems integration, specifications
Construction    → Detailed drawings, specifications, tender
Construction    → Site supervision, RFIs, shop drawing review
Administration
Post-occupancy  → Evaluation, snagging, lessons learned
```

### Fee structures
- **Percentage of construction cost:** 8–15% typical, varies by project type and complexity
- **Lump sum:** Fixed fee for defined scope. Risk on architect if scope grows.
- **Time-based:** Hourly/daily rate. Risk on client if project extends.
- **Hybrid:** Lump sum for early phases, time-based for construction administration

### Key deliverables by phase
- Schematic: concept sketches, massing models, area schedule, preliminary cost estimate
- Design Development: coordinated drawings, outline specifications, updated cost plan
- Construction Documents: full drawing set, specifications, structural/MEP coordination
- Construction Admin: site visits, RFI responses, shop drawing approvals, progress certificates

### Current trends in practice (2025)
- BIM (Building Information Modeling) is now mandatory on most large projects
- Integrated project delivery (IPD) — contractor involved from early design
- Digital fabrication blurring design/construction boundary
- AI-assisted design generation (Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, Spline, Hypar)
- Carbon accounting becoming standard in project reporting

## 2. Interior design

### Design scales
- **Space planning:** Functional organization, circulation, zone allocation
- **Interior architecture:** Structural interventions, built-ins, material palette
- **Furniture & fixtures:** FF&E (Furniture, Fixtures & Equipment) specification
- **Lighting design:** Natural + artificial, layers (ambient/task/accent)
- **Styling & art:** Decorative elements, artwork, plants, objects

### Material specification principles
- **Durability hierarchy:** Public/high-traffic → commercial grade; residential → lighter spec
- **Maintenance reality:** A beautiful material that can't be maintained has failed
- **Tactile experience:** Materials must feel right, not just look right — texture, weight, temperature
- **Acoustic consideration:** Hard surfaces = live acoustics; soft = dead. Most spaces need both.
- **Light interaction:** How does this material look at 7am vs 7pm? In direct sun vs overcast?

### Residential interior typologies
- Primary residence: permanence, personalization, evolves with owner
- High-end residential: statement pieces, bespoke joinery, art integration
- Show apartments: aspirational but producible, photography-ready
- Short-term rental: durable, distinctive, photogenic, easy to clean

### Commercial interior typologies
- Office: activity-based working, biophilic elements, collaboration vs focus zones
- Hospitality (hotel): arrival sequence, public vs private transition, brand expression
- F&B (restaurant/cafe): cover count optimization, atmosphere creation, kitchen efficiency
- Retail: customer journey, product display hierarchy, conversion optimization

### Current interior design trends (2025)
- Quiet luxury: understated, tactile, quality over logo
- Biophilic integration: living walls, natural materials, natural light optimization
- Curved forms and organic shapes replacing hard rectangular geometry
- Japandi (Japanese + Scandinavian): minimal, warm, crafted
- Maximalist rooms with strong point of view (reaction against generic minimalism)
- AI-generated mood boards and concept development

## 3. Urban planning & urbanism

### Planning scales
```
Regional planning    → Metropolitan area, infrastructure, land use policy
City planning        → Master plans, zoning, transportation networks
District planning    → Neighborhood character, mixed use, public space
Block planning       → Building heights, setbacks, ground floor activation
Street design        → Pedestrian realm, cycling, public furniture
```

### Key urbanism principles
**Jane Jacobs (still foundational):**
- Mixed uses at the block level create 24-hour vitality
- Short blocks create more pedestrian routes and connections
- Buildings of different ages (not just new) create economic diversity
- Concentration — density is not the enemy of livability

**New Urbanism:**
- Walkable neighborhoods with a clear center and edge
- Mixed uses and housing types
- Transit-oriented development
- Human-scaled streets and public spaces

**15-minute city:**
- All daily needs accessible within 15-minute walk or cycle
- Mixed-use zoning at neighborhood scale
- Reduces car dependency

### Public space design
- Activate edges — people sit at the edges, not the middle
- Define space — enclosure (buildings, trees, grade change) makes space feel like a room
- Allow for multiple uses — the best public spaces work for different groups at different times
- Maintenance is design — a beautiful space that isn't maintained communicates neglect

### Current urbanism trends (2025)
- Post-pandemic urban recovery — reimagining city centers post-remote work
- Climate adaptation — flood resilience, urban heat island mitigation, green infrastructure
- Electric mobility — bike infrastructure, EV charging, micro-mobility integration
- Tactical urbanism — temporary interventions proving concepts before permanent investment
- AI-assisted master planning and traffic simulation

## 4. Landscape architecture

### Design scales
- Site design: gardens, plazas, courtyards, roof terraces
- Park design: neighborhood parks, regional parks, linear parks
- Urban landscape: street trees, rain gardens, green corridors
- Ecological restoration: habitat creation, wetlands, brownfield remediation

### Planting design principles
- **Ecological fit:** Right plant, right place — climate, soil, water, light
- **Seasonal interest:** Design for all four seasons, not just summer
- **Layering:** Canopy → understorey → shrub → ground plane → ground cover
- **Maintenance realism:** A planting scheme that can't be maintained will be stripped out
- **Wildlife value:** Biodiversity is now a design requirement in most contexts

### Sustainable landscape
- **Rain gardens (bioswales):** Manage stormwater runoff, filter pollutants, create habitat
- **Permeable paving:** Reduce run-off, recharge groundwater
- **Native planting:** Lower maintenance, higher wildlife value, climate-appropriate
- **Green roofs:** Insulation, stormwater management, biodiversity, amenity

## 5. Sustainable & biophilic design

### Sustainability frameworks
- **LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design):** US-origin, globally used
- **BREEAM (Building Research Establishment Environmental Assessment Method):** UK-origin
- **WELL Building Standard:** Focus on human health and wellbeing
- **Living Building Challenge:** Net-positive energy, water, and waste
- **Passive House (Passivhaus):** Ultra-low energy through fabric-first approach

### Key sustainability strategies
**Energy:**
- Passive first (orientation, insulation, shading) before active systems
- Embodied carbon now as important as operational carbon
- Mass timber reducing structural embodied carbon
- Building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV)

**Water:**
- Rainwater harvesting
- Greywater recycling
- Drought-tolerant landscaping

**Materials:**
- Circular economy principles — design for disassembly
- Reclaimed and recycled materials
- Locally sourced to reduce transport carbon
- Avoiding high-VOC materials (indoor air quality)

### Biophilic design principles
**The 14 patterns of biophilic design:**
1. Visual connection with nature
2. Non-visual connection with nature (sound, smell, touch)
3. Non-rhythmic sensory stimuli (wind in trees, dancing light)
4. Thermal and airflow variability
5. Presence of water
6. Dynamic and diffuse light
7. Connection with natural systems (seasons, weather)
8. Biomorphic forms and patterns
9. Material connection with nature
10. Complexity and order (fractal patterns)
11. Prospect (views out over a landscape)
12. Refuge (sheltered, protected spaces)
13. Mystery (partially revealed information drawing you forward)
14. Risk/peril (controlled exposure to heights, edges)

## 6. Parametric & computational design

### Tools landscape (2025)
- **Rhino + Grasshopper:** Industry standard for parametric design
- **Revit:** BIM standard for documentation and coordination
- **Dynamo:** Visual programming for Revit
- **SpaceMaker (Autodesk):** AI-assisted urban massing
- **Hypar:** Cloud-based generative building design
- **Spline:** 3D for web and product design
- **Midjourney / Stable Diffusion:** Concept visualization and mood boarding
- **AI image tools with ControlNet:** Design iteration with architectural references

### Parametric design principles
- Define the rules, not the form — parametric design generates form from rules
- Parameters must map to design intent — "wall thickness varies with solar exposure"
- Bake the solution at the right time — too early loses flexibility, too late wastes computation
- Document the logic — parametric models are only useful if the logic is understandable

### AI in design workflow (2025)
- Concept: Midjourney/SD for rapid visual exploration of directions
- Massing: Spacemaker, Hypar for automated option generation
- Planning: AI analysis of solar, wind, pedestrian flow
- Documentation: AI-assisted specification writing
- Presentation: AI video/render generation
- Construction: AI-based schedule optimization and clash detection

## 7. Project typologies

- Museums, galleries (sequence, lighting, accessibility, expansion capacity)
- Libraries (evolving from book storage to community anchor)
- Schools (learning environment research-driven design)

### Hospitality
- Hotels (brand expression, efficiency, memorable arrival)
- Restaurants (atmosphere creation, operational efficiency, acoustics)
- Resorts (sense of place, landscape integration, local material)

## 8. Design philosophy

### The great architects' principles (applicable to all design)

**Mies van der Rohe:** "Less is more." Reduction to the essential. Every element earns its place.

**Louis Kahn:** "What does the building want to be?" Listen to the inherent nature of the program and materials.

**Tadao Ando:** Light as material. Silence as space. Concrete as texture. The spiritual in the everyday.

**Rem Koolhaas/OMA:** Program as generator. Generic as strategy. Bigness has its own logic.

**Zaha Hadid:** Fluid geometry. The end of the right angle. Landscape and building as continuous surface.

**Kengo Kuma:** "Erasing architecture." Building that disappears into landscape, light, and material texture.

**Bjarke Ingels (BIG):** "Yes is more." Pragmatic utopianism. Sustainability is not a constraint but a design driver.

### Principles that apply across all architecture

**Context first.** Every building exists in a place, a culture, a climate, a time. Ignoring context produces objects, not architecture.

**The section is the experience.** Plan is organization; section is spatial experience — the ceiling heights, the views, the light. Design the section.

**Material honesty.** Materials should express what they are. Fake stone is different from real stone, and the difference matters.

**Light is the material.** Every space is transformed by how it receives and handles light. Design the light, not just the shell.

**Detail is truth.** The quality of a building is ultimately revealed in its details. Where materials meet, where edges resolve, where the building touches the ground.
